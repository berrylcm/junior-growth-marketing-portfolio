## Marketing Against the Grain
**Date:** April 10, 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/what-200-b2b-decision-makers-told-us-lq4sc/

This week we asked you what actually builds trust in AI search results. The responses were clear: recognizable brands, credible sources, and detailed comparisons. Now here is what 200 B2B decision-makers told us when we asked the same question and where the data gets interesting.

Because it turns out, what makes buyers trust an AI answer and what makes them click a brand within it are two completely different things.

The Data
What builds trust in an AI-generated answer:

41% — Mentions of recognizable brands or companies
40% — Clear source citations they can verify
37% — Detailed explanations of pros and cons

What drives a click on a specific brand within that answer:

43% — The AI answer mentioned a specific feature they need
43% — The brand name was already familiar to them
39% — The AI answer included pricing or cost information
29% — The source cited looked credible

The Key Takeaway: Trust and Action Are Driven by Different Signals
This is the insight most marketers miss.

Trust stays with the answer. It does not automatically transfer to the recommendation. A buyer might fully trust an AI answer because it cites credible sources, but they will click on whichever brand has the feature they need or the price that works for their budget.

Consider the citation gap: 40% say verifiable citations build trust in an AI answer, but only 29% say source credibility is what drives them to click a specific brand. The signal that validates the answer is not the same signal that motivates engagement with a brand inside it.

This means your content strategy has to do two different jobs at the same time.

What This Means for Your Marketing
Content and Brand Content that gets cited as a source builds trust in AI answers. Content that highlights specific features and pricing drives clicks within them. A brand producing only one type is winning half the battle while losing the other.

Social Your social content feeds AI's source material. Posts that establish credibility help AI answers seem trustworthy. Posts that highlight specific capabilities help your brand get clicked within those answers. Both matter, but they matter differently.

Paid and Performance The 39% who click based on pricing are essentially pre-qualified on budget. They are not clicking to discover the price. They are clicking because the price already worked. Retargeting this segment may show higher conversion rates than you expect.

Email AI-discovered leads may arrive with pricing expectations already set by whatever the AI surfaced. If AI framed your cost differently than you would, your email sequence may need to reframe: adding context, explaining value, or clarifying what is included before the ask.

The Bigger Picture
The brands winning in AI search right now are not just the ones with the most optimized content. They are the ones with the strongest reputations built before anyone ever searched.

43% of B2B buyers say brand familiarity is what makes them click through from an AI recommendation. You cannot optimize your way to that. You have to earn it, through consistent presence, credible content, and a brand that people already recognize when AI surfaces it.

The content strategy comes second. The reputation comes first.

## Kevin Indig
**Date:** April 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kevinindig_the-seo-assumption-that-comprehensive-content-activity-7449792336324259841-7kHC/

The SEO assumption that comprehensive content performs better is completely wrong when it comes to ChatGPT citations.

Every "best practice" pushes you toward more: more subtopics, more sections, more words.

We analyzed 815,000 query and page pairs across 16,851 queries to see if this holds true for ChatGPT. The data says otherwise.

The "ultimate guide" strategy produces worse results than a focused article that covers two to three related angles well. In fact, moderate coverage of 26 to 50 percent of subtopics outperforms exhaustive coverage. Pages that cover everything score lower than pages that cover a quarter of the subtopics.

The pages that win consistently in ChatGPT are highly focused. They:

1. Match the query directly in their headings
2. Tend to be shorter, hitting a citation sweet spot between 500 and 2,000 words
3. Maintain structure, using 7 to 20 subheadings to organize content without diluting it

The takeaway: Build the page that is the best answer to one question. Not the page that adequately answers twenty.

As always, I've been spending a lot of time analyzing the impact of some recent Google updates.

The January 20th unconfirmed update was still the most interesting update of the year to me so far. I'm still working on March Core Update analysis, by the way. (Stay tuned!)

Looking at impacted sites from January, I'm still finding new examples that all match the same patterns.

A few things seemed to happen:

- the impact was mostly isolated to a specific subfolder, usually the blog

- the rest of the site was not hit nearly as hard (including commercial / transactional pages)

- within the targeted subfolder, the affected sites were playing SEO/GEO games (scaled AI content, self promoting listicles, artificial updates, misusing AggregateRating schema across pages, scaled comparison/alternative pages)

- there was lots of SEO growth before the crash, which is very common with Google updates

- the update seems to have disproportionately hit SaaS companies (who are also probably disproportionately trying these tactics)

By *no* means did Google demote all the sites doing these things, but I wonder if they were testing an algorithmic change across the most obvious examples.

FWIW - I would NOT consider a site "safe" if it's doing all the above things and hasn't been hit yet.

Use AI to move faster through research, outlining, and drafting. Then invest the time saved into incorporating expert insights, proprietary data, and other elements that make your content stand out.
